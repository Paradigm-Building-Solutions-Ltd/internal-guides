Anthropic (the company that makes Claude) has built an official connector for Microsoft 365 that hooks Claude into SharePoint, OneDrive, Outlook, and Teams — and it's already included in our existing Team plan at no extra cost.

01
Plain English first

The words people will throw around — what they actually mean.

Before going anywhere, here are the four bits of jargon that show up everywhere in this conversation. Once these click, the rest is easy.

Microsoft 365 / SharePoint
M365 is Microsoft's bundle of work apps — Outlook (email), Teams (chat & meetings), OneDrive (your personal cloud files), SharePoint (the company-wide shared file system and intranet), Word, Excel, PowerPoint.
Think: our company's "digital office" — wherever a file, email, or chat lives at PGC, it's somewhere inside M365.
Microsoft Entra Tenant
A "tenant" is our company's private Microsoft account hub. It's the central system that holds every PGC employee's Microsoft login, what they can access, and what permissions they have. Entra is the new name for what used to be called Azure AD.
Think: a digital filing cabinet that holds every PGC login. When Sarah types her work email and password into Outlook, she's signing into our tenant.
Microsoft Graph API
An "API" is just a way for two programs to talk to each other. Microsoft Graph is the official phone line that other apps use to ask Microsoft 365 for information — emails, files, calendar events, chats.
Think: when Claude wants to fetch a file from our SharePoint, it calls Microsoft Graph and says "hey, can you get me this?" — and Microsoft decides whether to hand it over.
MCP (Model Context Protocol)
A new open standard for connecting AI tools (like Claude) to business apps (like SharePoint) in a clean, safe, predictable way. Created by Anthropic, now used by other AI companies too.
Think: like USB-C for AI — one standard plug that works across many tools. Without MCP, every connection would be a custom hack. With MCP, Claude can plug into SharePoint the same way it plugs into Slack or Google Drive.
Delegated Permissions
A type of permission where the app (Claude) only ever acts on behalf of the person signed in — and can never do anything that person can't already do themselves.
Think: Claude is like a research assistant standing next to you. If you can open a folder, Claude can read it for you. If a folder is locked to you, it stays locked to Claude too.
Global Administrator
The most senior admin role in our Microsoft tenant — the only person who can approve big tenant-wide changes like installing a new third-party app for the whole company.
Think: Dave (or whoever holds that role at the IT contractor). They're the only ones who can say "yes, this app is allowed into our PGC Microsoft world."
02
The headliner

The official Microsoft 365 Connector — what we should actually use.

SIDE NOTE Already built Anthropic shipped this connector as a real product, not a beta. It's listed in the Microsoft Marketplace. We're not pioneering anything here — we're using something thousands of companies already run.

This is the one. Built by Anthropic, supported by Anthropic, designed to do exactly what we want — let Claude search and read across our Microsoft world, safely, with proper guardrails. No additional money. No additional software.

What it actually does

Once it's turned on, anyone at PGC who connects their Microsoft account can ask Claude things like:

  • →"Find the Q4 strategic planning document in SharePoint."
  • →"Summarize the email thread with the lumber supplier about delivery delays."
  • →"What did we discuss in the Teams channel about the Buildforth site visit?"
  • →"Pull meeting notes from last week's leadership sync."

Claude figures out which Microsoft service to look in, fetches what it needs, and answers with citations back to the source files or emails. The person asking doesn't have to download anything, upload anything, or paste anything.

What it absolutely cannot do

This is the part to emphasise. The connector is read-only. That word matters. Claude through this connector cannot:

  • ✗Send an email or reply to one
  • ✗Edit, create, or delete a file in SharePoint or OneDrive
  • ✗Add or move a calendar event
  • ✗Post a message in Teams
  • ✗Change any sharing permission anywhere
  • ✗See anything the signed-in user couldn't see directly in Microsoft
"If a SharePoint folder is locked to you in Microsoft, it stays locked to Claude when it's acting on your behalf. — What "delegated permissions" really mean

What needs to happen to turn it on

Two things, both one-time. After this, it just works.

STEP A · MICROSOFT SIDE
Dave grants tenant consent

Dave (as Global Administrator) signs into the Microsoft admin centre and approves the connector for our whole tenant. This installs two small "service apps" inside our Microsoft account so the connection can exist. Takes about 5 minutes. He only does this once, ever.

STEP B · CLAUDE SIDE
I enable it on our Team plan

As a Claude organisation Owner, I go into Org Settings > Connectors and add Microsoft 365 to our team. After that, each person at PGC who wants to use it goes to their own settings and clicks "Connect" to link their Microsoft account.

After Steps A and B, individual users can connect their own Microsoft accounts in their own Claude settings. They don't see the admin consent screen — just a quick sign-in. Done.

One important caveat to know

The connector searches across our whole SharePoint tenant, scoped to whatever each user can already see. We can't tell the connector "only look in these specific SharePoint sites and ignore the others." If we want certain SharePoint sites to be invisible to Claude entirely, the way to do that is on the SharePoint side — not by configuring Claude. Worth a quick conversation with Dave about whether any of our SharePoint sites have content sensitive enough that we'd want to mark them excluded at the SharePoint level.

04
Worth its own section

Claude inside Excel, PowerPoint, Word & Outlook — a different beast, a great pairing.

These are separate from the M365 Connector — but they pair beautifully with it. The connector lets Claude find stuff across our M365 world. The Office add-ins let Claude work inside a specific document you have open. Together: complete coverage.

What "Office add-ins" means in plain English

Don't worry, it's simpler than it sounds

An "add-in" is just a little extra panel that lives inside an existing app. When you install Claude's add-in for Excel, the next time you open Excel a Claude chat panel appears on the right side of the screen. You can type questions about the specific spreadsheet open in front of you — and Claude can read the cells, do analysis, and even write changes back into the workbook.

Same idea for PowerPoint, Word, and Outlook. There's a Claude sidebar inside each one. You stay in the Office app the whole time — no copy-pasting between Claude.ai and Excel.

What Claude in Excel can do

Ask questions about specific cells, formulas, or whole sheets. Build pivot tables and analysis. Spot trends, errors, or outliers. Add columns of calculated values. Reformat data. Pull data from one tab and summarise on another. Citations show exactly which cells Claude referenced.

"Look at the budget tab and tell me which line items are over by more than 10%."

What Claude in PowerPoint can do

Generate full editable slides from a PDF, an Excel file, or a website URL — and the slides come out in our branded template (Catamaran, PGC colours, the right layouts) if we set the template before generating. Restructure existing decks. Tighten messaging. Rewrite for tone.

"Take this StokedLiving project brief and turn it into a 6-slide investor update using our deck template."

What Claude in Word can do

Read, rewrite, and edit Word documents in place. Apply tone changes (more formal, more direct). Insert summaries. Cross-reference with other open Office files (if cross-file context is turned on). Generate first drafts from prompts.

"Rewrite this contract draft so the language matches our Paradigm brand voice."

What Claude in Outlook can do

Read the email or thread you currently have open and help draft replies, summarise long threads, extract action items, or analyse tone. Note: it only sees the email you're looking at, not your whole inbox (the connector is the one that searches across all email).

"Summarise this 40-email thread with the building inspector and list every open question."

How we install these — the actual steps

Free from the Microsoft Marketplace
The individual route (per person)
  1. Open Excel (or PowerPoint, Word, Outlook) on Mac or Windows desktop.
  2. In the ribbon, click Home → Add-ins (Windows) or Insert → Add-ins (Mac).
  3. Search for "Claude by Anthropic".
  4. Click Add. The Claude panel appears in the sidebar.
  5. Sign in with the same Claude account that's tied to our PGC Team plan.
  6. Repeat in each Office app — they're separate installs but quick.
The deploy-to-everyone route (Dave's call)
  1. Dave goes to the Microsoft 365 Admin Centre.
  2. Goes to Settings → Org Settings → User-owned apps and services and confirms "Let users access the Office Store" is on.
  3. Goes to Settings → Integrated apps → Add-ins.
  4. Searches the Microsoft AppSource for "Claude by Anthropic".
  5. Deploys the add-ins to specific users or the whole org in one click.
  6. Users open Office, sign in to Claude once — done.

The "work across files" superpower

Once you've installed Claude in more than one Office app, there's a setting called "Work across files" (in Settings inside any of the add-ins). Turn it on, and Claude can read context from every open Office file at once. So you could have an Excel workbook and a PowerPoint open at the same time, and ask Claude in PowerPoint to "pull the Q2 numbers from the budget workbook and turn them into a summary slide." It does that without you copy-pasting anything.

Note: this cross-app context only works for files that are open at the same time. Closed files don't count.

An important admin note for our setup

For Team and Enterprise plans like ours, an organisation Owner (me/you) needs to flip a toggle in Claude's Org Settings called "Office agents" to allow the add-ins to work for our team. Without that toggle on, people will install the add-in but it won't connect properly. One-click thing. I'll handle it when we're ready.

The fine print

What the connector actually asks for — and why each thing matters.

When the connector is approved, it asks Microsoft for permission to read specific kinds of data. Here's the full list in human terms, grouped sensibly. Dave will look at this carefully — and we can strip out anything we don't want, individually.

Category
What the technical permissions are
What it means in practice
Identity & sign-in
User.Read, openid, email, profile, offline_access
Lets Claude confirm who is signed in and keep the session going. Standard stuff — every Microsoft-connected app needs this.
Email (Outlook)
Mail.Read, Mail.ReadBasic, Mail.Read.Shared, MailboxFolder.Read, MailboxItem.Read
Lets Claude read the user's emails and any shared mailboxes they already have access to. Includes archives. Can't send, reply, or delete.
Calendar
Calendars.Read, Calendars.Read.Shared
Lets Claude see your meetings and calendars shared with you. Can't add, move, or cancel anything.
Teams chat
Chat.Read, Chat.ReadBasic, ChatMember.Read, ChatMessage.Read
Lets Claude read your 1:1 and group chats in Teams. Only ones you're part of.
Teams channels
Channel.ReadBasic.All, ChannelMessage.Read.All
Lets Claude read messages in Teams channels you're a member of. Private channels you're not in stay invisible.
Meetings
OnlineMeetings.Read, OnlineMeetingTranscript.Read.All, OnlineMeetingAiInsight.Read, OnlineMeetingArtifact.Read.All, OnlineMeetingRecording.Read.All
Lets Claude read meeting transcripts, AI summaries, and recordings. This is the most sensitive one — we should ask Dave whether to leave it on or strip it.
Files (SharePoint & OneDrive)
Files.Read, Files.Read.All, Sites.Read.All
The core one. Lets Claude search and read files across SharePoint sites and OneDrive folders the user has access to.

User.ReadBasic.All
Lets Claude look up basic profile info of other people in our org — used mainly for finding meeting availability when someone asks "when can John and I both meet?"

The two big admin levers

Dave can pull either or both of these in Microsoft's admin console — they're independent dials.

LEVER 1
Restrict who can use it

In Microsoft Entra, Dave can set "Assignment required = Yes" on the connector apps. Then only people he specifically assigns can connect. Easy way to start with a small pilot group and expand later.

LEVER 2
Strip which permissions are allowed

In the same console, Dave can revoke any individual permission from the list above. Don't want meeting recordings exposed? Revoke OnlineMeetingRecording.Read.All. The Claude tool that would use it just stops working.

07
The "is it safe" question

Privacy, security, and what happens to our data — the clean version.

These are the points worth stating plainly when the safety question comes up. They're not marketing — they're how the system actually works.

A.
Claude can never exceed our existing Microsoft permissions

If our SharePoint already locks a folder away from someone, Claude is also locked out when acting for that person. It inherits, never escalates.

B.
Read-only, full stop

No writes, no edits, no sends, no deletes. Nothing in Microsoft can be modified through this connector. Worst case: Claude reads something it could already have read manually.

C.
Anthropic doesn't store our M365 data

When someone asks a question, Claude fetches what it needs through Microsoft, answers, and that data isn't kept around afterwards.

D.
No AI training on our content

Team plan default — Anthropic doesn't use our conversations or our M365 data to train their AI models.

E.
On-demand only

The connector doesn't continuously scan our data in the background. It only reaches into Microsoft when a user actively asks a question that needs it.

F.
Microsoft's own safety nets still apply

Existing data loss prevention, sensitivity labels, and sharing rules in our M365 keep working. Claude's traffic flows through Microsoft Graph, so those policies still govern it.

G.
Revocable at any time

Three independent kill switches: any user can disconnect themselves; we can turn the connector off for our whole organisation in Claude; Dave can yank consent on the Microsoft side. Any one of those cuts access.

H.
Fully auditable

Because everything runs through Microsoft Graph with each user's identity, Dave can see exactly what was queried, by whom, and when — in our existing Microsoft audit logs.

How I'd actually do this

A phased rollout we can start as soon as Dave gives the green light.

I'd resist turning this on for everyone at once. Better to run a small pilot, prove it out, learn the quirks specific to our SharePoint structure, then expand. Two phases.

Pilot
01
Small group, real workflows
Weeks 1–3 after Dave approves
  1. Dave grants the one-time Microsoft tenant consent (5 minutes).
  2. Dave restricts who can use it to a small pilot group via "Assignment required" in Microsoft Entra.
  3. If we want to strip specific permissions (e.g. meeting recordings), Dave revokes those now.
  4. I enable the connector on the Claude organisation side.
  5. Pilot group: me, you (Amy), one person from PGC, one from Paradigm Solar, one from Buildforth or StokedLiving — five people total.
  6. Each person connects their own Microsoft account and uses it in real work for two weeks.
  7. We track what worked, what surprised us, what made anyone uncomfortable.
  8. Install Claude in Excel and PowerPoint add-ins for the same pilot group while we're at it.
Expansion
02
Broader rollout, with guardrails
Weeks 4–8
  1. Document an internal usage policy — what to put in prompts, what not to, what to do if Claude shows you something you didn't expect to be able to see.
  2. Quick training: a 30-minute session per division on how to use this without surprises.
  3. Dave expands the assignment in Microsoft Entra to the full team or specific departments based on what we learned.
  4. Roll out the Office add-ins more broadly through Dave's central deployment.
  5. Set a calendar reminder to review usage and permissions at 60 and 120 days.
  6. Plan a more formal SharePoint hygiene pass with Dave before opening to everyone — make sure our permissions structure is what we think it is.

  • Claude for Chrome on SharePoint — too risky, too new, not worth it
