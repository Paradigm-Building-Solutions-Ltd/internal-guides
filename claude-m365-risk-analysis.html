For leadership review / Connecting Claude to Microsoft 365 and SharePoint

Is this safe for Paradigm to turn on?

QuestionCan we connect Claude to our Microsoft 365 safely
Overall riskLow to moderate, manageable
RecommendationYes, with a pilot and free controls
Facts verified againstOfficial Anthropic, Microsoft, and Privacy Commissioner of Canada sources
If you only read this

Anthropic, the company behind Claude, offers an official connector that lets Claude search and read our Microsoft 365 content. It is read-only, so Claude can find and summarize information but can never change, send, or delete anything. It only ever sees what the signed-in person can already see, and only when that person asks a question that needs it.

On our paid plan, Anthropic does not use our data to train its models and does not keep copies of our files. The one genuine consideration is that, like most cloud tools we already use, the data is processed and stored on servers in the United States. Using a United States provider is legal for a Canadian company as long as we have the right contract in place and are transparent about it, which we can be.

For our most sensitive material, finance, human resources, and proprietary manufacturing methods, we can wall it off from Claude entirely. There are free ways to do this, and one very cheap paid way that costs about $488 per year for the whole company. My recommendation is to turn this on for a small pilot, with the walls in place first, and expand once we are comfortable.

01
What we are actually turning on

A read-only assistant, not a new door into our data.

We are turning on the official Microsoft 365 connector built by Anthropic. It is a finished, supported product, and it is included in the Claude plan we already pay for, at no extra cost.

Once it is on, a staff member who links their own Microsoft account can ask Claude things like "find the Q4 planning document" or "summarize the email thread with the lumber supplier." Claude finds the file or email, reads it, and answers, with links back to the source. The person does not have to download, upload, or paste anything.

The most important thing to understand is what it cannot do. The connector is read-only:

  • It cannot send an email or reply to one
  • It cannot create, edit, or delete a file in SharePoint or OneDrive
  • It cannot add or change a calendar event
  • It cannot post in Teams or change any permission or setting
  • It cannot see anything the signed-in person could not already open themselves

In other words, the connector gives Claude no access that our staff do not already have. It lets Claude look, on a person's behalf, at what that person could already look at.

02
Where our data goes, and what is actually stored

The honest answer on data, storage, and training.

This is the section that matters most for a risk decision, so I have kept it plain and complete. Every point here is verified against Anthropic's official documentation.

Is our data used to train Claude?

No. On our paid commercial plan, Anthropic does not use our conversations or our Microsoft 365 content to train its models. This is part of their commercial terms and is the default, not a setting we switch on. The recent change that allows training on personal data applies only to individual consumer accounts, never to business plans like ours.

What actually travels to Claude, and is the file stored there?

This is worth getting exactly right, because it is easy to misunderstand. When someone asks a question, Claude does not receive a pre-made summary from Microsoft. The actual relevant content of the file or email is sent to Claude's systems so the model can read it and answer. That is how any AI assistant works. The important part is what happens next: that content is used to produce the answer and is then cleaned up. The connector does not cache or store file content.

So the accurate picture is:

  • The real content is transmitted to Claude's systems to be read, in the moment, to answer the question
  • The file itself is never copied into permanent storage at Anthropic
  • If the answer is saved as part of a chat, that chat may quote some of the content, and it lives in the user's chat history until they delete it, then is removed from Anthropic's systems within 30 days

The practical takeaway: the only Paradigm content that persists at Anthropic is whatever sits inside saved chats. There is no growing copy of our SharePoint or our mailboxes on Claude's servers. This matters a lot for the breach question in chapter 7.

Where is the data processed and stored?

On servers in the United States. Anthropic may route traffic through other regions, but storage for our type of plan is in the US. A Canadian-hosted option exists only for a different, developer-style deployment, not for the product this connector runs on, and even a Canadian location would not remove the fact that Anthropic is a United States company subject to United States law. This is the same situation as most cloud tools we already use, and chapter 6 covers whether it is legal for us (it is, with conditions).

Can Anthropic or Claude "see" our information?

To answer a question, Claude's systems do read the relevant text in order to process it. That is true of every AI assistant, and it means this is not a system where Anthropic can never technically see the content. What protects it: strong encryption in transit and storage, Anthropic's security certifications, the no-training guarantee, restricted internal access, and the 30-day deletion of saved chats. I would rather state that plainly than overstate the protection.

In short

Not used for training. Files not copied or kept. The actual content is read in the moment to answer, then cleaned up. Encrypted in transit (TLS 1.2 or higher) and at rest (AES-256). Stored in the United States. Only saved chats persist, and they are removed within 30 days of deletion. Strong, audited controls, but not a zero-knowledge system.

03
The safety guarantees we can rely on

Five protections that are built in by design.

  • It inherits permissions, it does not expand them. Claude acts on behalf of the signed-in person and can only reach what that person can already reach. If a folder is locked to someone in Microsoft, it stays locked to Claude for that person.
  • Read-only. Nothing in Microsoft can be changed, sent, or deleted through the connector. The worst case is that Claude reads something a person could already have read.
  • On-demand only. The connector does not sit in the background scanning our SharePoint. It only reaches in when a person asks a question that needs it.
  • Revocable at three independent levels. Any user can disconnect their own account; we can switch the connector off for the whole company in Claude; and our Microsoft administrator can withdraw consent on the Microsoft side. Any one cuts access immediately.
  • Fully auditable. Because everything runs through Microsoft with each person's identity, our administrator can see what was queried, by whom, and when, in our existing Microsoft logs.

05
Walling off our crown jewels

Every way to block Claude from sensitive sites, free and paid.

This is the chapter I researched most carefully, because it is where a wrong assumption could cost us money or leave a gap. Here is the full toolkit, verified against both Anthropic and Microsoft documentation, sorted from free to paid.

First, what does not work

A common assumption is that we can give Claude access to some SharePoint sites and not others by adjusting Microsoft permissions site by site for the connector. Anthropic's own documentation states clearly that this site-by-site approach is not supported for this connector, because the search it uses looks across the whole tenant. We should not rely on it.

The free hard blocks, step by step

Free option A: turn off "Allow this site to appear in search results"

Every SharePoint site has a built-in setting that removes it from search. Microsoft's own documentation confirms that turning it off "blocks the site content from showing up in both the organization-wide search and the site-specific search." Because Claude finds content through that search, this stops Claude from reaching it.

  1. In the site, go to Settings, then Site Settings, then Search and offline availability.
  2. Set "Allow this site to appear in Search results" to No, and save. (This can also be done in bulk with a PowerShell script for several sites at once.)

Trade-off to know: it removes the site from search for everyone, not just Claude, so staff also cannot find it through search and must navigate to it directly. For crown-jewel sites that is usually acceptable. It is a discovery block, not a permissions change.

Free option B: Restricted SharePoint Search (an allow-list)

This is a free Microsoft feature built for exactly this moment, letting an administrator approve a list of up to 100 sites that are allowed to appear in company-wide search and in AI agents like Claude. Everything not on the list is hidden from Claude.

  1. Our administrator turns on Restricted SharePoint Search (done with a short PowerShell command).
  2. They add only the safe, reviewed sites to the allowed list, and leave finance, HR, and manufacturing off it.
  3. It takes effect within about an hour.

Trade-offs to know, stated honestly: Microsoft describes this as a temporary measure, not a long-term solution. It is capped at 100 sites. It is not a security boundary, and it is slightly leaky, if a user recently opened a site or it was shared with them directly, it can still surface for that user. For a company our size it is a strong free interim wall, but it is not airtight.

My free recommendation

For Paradigm, combine two free controls and you get a strong result at zero cost: keep the crown-jewel sites out of search using option A or B (so Claude cannot find them), and tighten permissions so the people using Claude do not have access to those sites anyway (the only true boundary). Add the Organization Instruction soft block on top as a third layer. No license required.

The cheap paid hard block, and the licensing trick you asked about

Microsoft's Restricted Content Discovery is the cleanest deny-list: pick specific sites and they vanish from search, Copilot, and agents like Claude, while the people with access can still open the files directly. It is the purpose-built tool.

Yes, one license unlocks it for everyone

You asked whether buying it for one user unlocks it for the whole company. It does. Microsoft confirms that assigning a single Microsoft 365 Copilot license to one user unlocks SharePoint Advanced Management, including Restricted Content Discovery, for the entire tenant, and it can then be applied to any site for all users. This is the same one-license-unlocks-everything pattern that exists for several Microsoft features.

So the cheapest route to the proper hard block is to keep one Copilot license active, about $488 per year. We just cancelled all three Copilot licenses to save $1,465 per year, so keeping one still saves us roughly $980 per year while giving us the full deny-list control. There is also a standalone SharePoint Advanced Management add-on at about $3 per user per month if we prefer not to keep a Copilot license; I will confirm which is genuinely cheapest for us before any spend.

The strongest control of all, if we ever want it: encrypt the file

If we apply a sensitivity label that encrypts a file, Claude cannot read the content even if it somehow finds the file, unless the user has a specific "extract" right that we can withhold. The protection travels with the file wherever it goes. This is the gold standard for true crown-jewel material such as proprietary manufacturing methods. The catch is that it needs a higher Microsoft license tier than our current one, so it would be a deliberate upgrade for a small number of users, not a free option. Worth keeping on the table for the manufacturing IP specifically.

06
The soft block, explained properly

How we make Claude follow the rule every single time.

You asked exactly how the soft block works, step by step, and how we guarantee Claude uses it whenever it touches SharePoint. Here is the precise answer.

It is not a skill, it is an Organization Instruction

A "skill" in Claude is a capability that is pulled in only when it is relevant, so it is not guaranteed to apply to every conversation. That is the wrong tool for a rule that must always be on. The right tool is a feature called Organization Instructions, available to administrators on our Team plan. Anthropic's documentation states that organization instructions are "custom instructions that Claude follows in every conversation across your organization" and are "included in every message sent by everyone in your organization."

That phrase, every message by everyone, is the key. We write the rule once, and Claude receives it on every single query from every staff member automatically. We do not rely on anyone remembering to invoke anything.

How we would set it up

  1. As a Claude organization owner, I open Organization settings and set the Organization Instructions.
  2. The instruction names our sensitive sites and tells Claude never to read from them or use their content, even if a user asks, and to tell the user it is restricted instead.
  3. The instruction has a 3,000 character limit, which is plenty for a list of sites and a clear rule. Organization instructions also take precedence over anything an individual user sets.
Honest limit of the soft block

This is a high-reliability control because the rule is present in every message, but it is still guidance to the model, not a technical wall. A determined or clever prompt could in principle try to talk around it. That is why the soft block is a useful third layer, not a substitute for a free hard block on the truly sensitive sites. Use both together: the hard block stops Claude from finding the site at all, and the instruction tells Claude to refuse even if it ever did.

The layered approach I recommend

  • Layer 1, permissions: the people using Claude do not have access to crown-jewel sites. True boundary, free.
  • Layer 2, search wall: crown-jewel sites are removed from search (free option A or B), so Claude cannot find them.
  • Layer 3, instruction: Organization Instruction tells Claude to refuse those sites by name. Free.
  • Optional Layer 4, deny-list: Restricted Content Discovery for about $488 per year if we want the purpose-built tool and tenant-wide auditing.
  • Optional Layer 5, encryption: sensitivity-label encryption on the manufacturing IP files for content that must never be readable. License upgrade.
07
The legal question

Is it lawful for a Canadian company to use a United States provider?

Short answer: yes, it is lawful, and it is something Canadian companies do every day with tools like Microsoft, Google, and others. There are conditions we must meet, and they are straightforward. The detail below reflects Canadian privacy law as published by the Office of the Privacy Commissioner of Canada.

Important

I am the IT and AI Coordinator, not a lawyer. This section is a careful plain-language summary to inform the decision. For anything we want to rely on formally, we should have it confirmed by legal counsel. The stakes warrant a quick review.

What the law actually requires

Under Canada's federal privacy law (PIPEDA), and British Columbia's provincial law (PIPA), a company is allowed to send personal information to a service provider in another country for processing. There is no rule that the data must stay in Canada. What the law does require is the following:

  • Comparable protection by contract. We must ensure the provider protects the data to a level comparable to what it would get here, through a written agreement. Anthropic offers a data processing agreement for exactly this, and holds recognized security certifications.
  • Transparency. We must be open with our people that their information may be processed and stored outside Canada and could be subject to foreign laws. In practice this is a line in our privacy notice and our internal policy.
  • Accountability. We remain responsible for the data even while Anthropic processes it. We choose the provider carefully, keep the agreement, and apply sensible controls, which is what this whole analysis is.

The one thing we cannot contract away

No contract can override the laws of another country. While our data sits on United States servers, it is, in principle, subject to United States legal processes such as government requests. This is true of every United States cloud tool we use today, not unique to Claude. The realistic mitigation is the same as everywhere else in this document: keep the genuinely sensitive material out of Claude entirely, so there is little of consequence on those servers in the first place.

What we should do to be clean

  • Put Anthropic's data processing agreement in place as part of enabling the connector.
  • Add a short, clear line to our employee and customer privacy notices that we use reputable third-party processors that may operate outside Canada.
  • Keep the crown-jewel controls from chapter 5 in place so the most sensitive personal and proprietary information never reaches a foreign server.
  • Have legal counsel give the arrangement a brief review before a wide rollout.
08
If something goes wrong

If Claude or Anthropic is breached, what happens to us?

These are the hard "what if" questions, answered directly.

If Claude reads one of our employee files, and Anthropic is later hacked, can our data leak?
Only what reached Anthropic and persisted there could be exposed, and that is a deliberately small surface. The connector does not store our files, so there is no copy of our HR folder or our mailboxes sitting on Claude's servers waiting to leak. The only Paradigm content that persists is what lives inside saved chat conversations, which may quote pieces of a file, plus the encrypted access tokens. So the honest answer is: a leak could expose the contents of saved chats that happened to discuss sensitive data, but not a wholesale copy of our SharePoint or email. We shrink this further by deleting chats that touch sensitive topics and by keeping crown jewels out of Claude in the first place.
Who carries the legal burden, Paradigm or Anthropic?
Both, but in different ways, and we cannot fully hand it off. Under Canadian privacy law, Paradigm remains accountable for our people's personal information even when a provider processes it. So if a breach at Anthropic exposed Paradigm data, we would be responsible for assessing the harm and, if it crossed the legal threshold of a real risk of significant harm, for reporting it to the Privacy Commissioner of Canada and notifying the affected people. At the same time, Anthropic, as our processor, is contractually responsible to us under its agreement, must notify us of a breach, and carries its own regulatory obligations. In plain terms: we cannot say "not our problem, it was Claude," but we can hold Anthropic to account under the contract and recover from them. This is exactly why the small data surface and the crown-jewel controls matter, they limit what we would ever be on the hook for.
Could a competitor or outsider pull our data through this connector?
Not without first logging in as one of our own staff. Access runs through Microsoft sign-in with our existing protections, including multi-factor authentication, and Claude only ever sees what that signed-in person can see. An outsider would need to defeat a staff member's Microsoft login, which is the same barrier that protects all our Microsoft data today.
When an employee leaves, is their Claude history with company data a loose end?
It can be, which is why we handle it in offboarding. Removing a leaver's Claude seat and clearing their access is part of the onboarding and offboarding process we are already building. It is a routine step, not a special problem.
Does Claude reading a file create a permanent copy somewhere?
No. The content is read in the moment to answer and then cleaned up. The only persistence is inside saved chats, which are deletable and are removed from Anthropic's systems within 30 days of deletion.
What is the single most effective thing we can do to limit all of this?
Data minimization. Keep finance, HR, and manufacturing secrets walled off from Claude using the free controls in chapter 5, and encourage staff to delete chats that touch sensitive material. If the sensitive data never reaches Claude, almost every risk in this document shrinks to near zero.

Outlook, specifically

What to know about email and the connector.

  • Read-only here too. Claude can read and summarize email but cannot send, reply, forward, or delete. This is stricter than the Google version of the connector, which can draft replies.
  • Shared mailboxes are included. If a person has access to a shared mailbox such as info@ or accounts@, Claude can read those messages for them as well. We confirm who has shared-mailbox access during the pilot.
  • Personal inboxes stay personal. Claude only reads the signed-in person's own mail and the shared mailboxes they already use. It cannot read a colleague's private inbox.
  • Archived mail is included. The read permission covers archived messages, not just the current inbox. Worth knowing for anything sensitive sitting in old email.
  • The same controls apply. If a topic is too sensitive for email exposure, the answer is the same as for files: minimize what is there, and remember the no-training and no-storage guarantees still hold.
10
My recommendation

Turn it on carefully, in two phases.

The risk is low to moderate and well understood, the controls are real, and the strongest of them are free. I recommend we proceed, starting small.

Before we switch anything on

  • Identify the exact finance, HR, and manufacturing sites to wall off.
  • Apply the free controls to those sites: remove them from search and confirm the pilot users do not have access to them.
  • Set the Organization Instruction naming those sites.
  • Decide whether to strip the meeting-recording permission from the start.
  • Put Anthropic's data processing agreement in place and add the privacy-notice line.

Phase one, a small pilot (about three weeks)

  • Our IT support partner grants the one-time Microsoft approval and limits the connector to a small pilot group.
  • Pilot group of about five people across the divisions, using it in real work.
  • We watch what works, what surprises us, and what makes anyone uncomfortable.

Phase two, broader rollout (weeks four to eight)

  • Write a short staff usage policy and run a 30-minute training per division.
  • Decide whether to add Restricted Content Discovery (about $488 per year) for purpose-built, audited deny-listing, or stay on the free controls.
  • Expand access with the sensitive-site walls confirmed in place, and have legal counsel give the arrangement a brief review.
Cost to the company

The connector is included in our existing plan at no extra cost, and the protections I recommend for the pilot are all free. The only optional spend is about $488 per year for Restricted Content Discovery if we later want the purpose-built deny-list, which still leaves us saving against what we paid for Copilot before.
