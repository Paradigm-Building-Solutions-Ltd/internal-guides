If you only read this

Anthropic, the company behind Claude, offers an official connector that lets Claude search and read our Microsoft 365 content. It is read-only, which means Claude can find and summarize information but can never change, send, or delete anything. It only ever sees what the signed-in person can already see, and it only reaches into Microsoft when someone asks a question that needs it.

Under our paid plan, Anthropic does not use our data to train its models, and it does not keep copies of our files. The one genuine consideration is that, like almost every cloud service, the data is processed and stored on servers in the United States, not Canada.

For our most sensitive material, such as financial records and proprietary manufacturing methods, there are concrete controls we can put in place so Claude can never reach them, even if a staff member asks. My recommendation is to turn this on for a small pilot group first, with those controls in place, and expand once we are comfortable.

01
What we are actually turning on

A read-only assistant, not a new door into our data.

We are turning on the official Microsoft 365 connector built by Anthropic. It is a finished, supported product, not an experiment, and it is included in the Claude plan we already pay for.

Once it is on, a staff member who links their own Microsoft account can ask Claude things like "find the Q4 planning document" or "summarize the email thread with the lumber supplier." Claude finds the right file or email, reads it, and answers, with links back to the source. The person does not have to download, upload, or paste anything.

The most important thing to understand is what it cannot do. The connector is read-only:

  • It cannot send an email or reply to one
  • It cannot create, edit, or delete a file in SharePoint or OneDrive
  • It cannot add or change a calendar event
  • It cannot post in Teams or change any permission or setting
  • It cannot see anything the signed-in person could not already open themselves

In other words, the connector does not give Claude any access that our staff do not already have. It lets Claude look, on a person's behalf, at things that person could already look at.

02
Where our data goes, and who can see it

The honest answer on data, storage, and training.

This is the section that matters most for a risk decision, so I have kept it plain and complete. Every point here is verified against Anthropic's official documentation.

Is our data used to train Claude?

No. On our paid commercial plan, Anthropic does not use our conversations or our Microsoft 365 content to train its models. This is part of their commercial terms and is the default, not a setting we have to switch on. The recent change that allows training on personal data applies only to individual consumer accounts, not to business plans like ours.

Does Anthropic keep copies of our files?

The connector does not store or cache the content of our files. Each request pulls only what is needed to answer the question and is cleaned up after the answer is given. The one nuance worth stating clearly: if the answer becomes part of a saved chat, that chat is kept in the user's history until they delete it, and is then removed from Anthropic's systems within 30 days. So the file itself is not copied, but a saved conversation that quoted from it lives in that chat until deleted.

Where is the data processed and stored?

On servers in the United States. Anthropic may route traffic through other regions, but storage for our type of plan is in the US. A Canadian-hosted option exists only for a different, developer-style deployment, not for the product this connector runs on, and even a Canadian location would not change the fact that Anthropic is a US company subject to US law. This is the same situation as most cloud tools we already use, but it is the one point I want leadership to be aware of rather than gloss over.

Can Anthropic or Claude "see" our information?

To answer a question, Claude's systems do read the relevant text in order to process it. That is true of every AI assistant. It is protected by strong encryption in transit and storage, by Anthropic's security certifications, by the no-training guarantee, and by the 30-day deletion of saved chats. It is not, however, a system where Anthropic can never technically see the content. I would rather state that plainly than overstate the protection.

In short

Not used for training. Files not copied. Encrypted in transit and at rest. Stored in the US. Deleted from saved chats within 30 days of deletion. Strong, audited security controls, but not a zero-knowledge system.

03
The safety guarantees we can rely on

Five protections that are built in by design.

  • It inherits permissions, it does not expand them. Claude acts on behalf of the signed-in person and can only reach what that person can already reach. If a folder is locked to someone in Microsoft, it stays locked to Claude for that person.
  • Read-only. Nothing in Microsoft can be changed, sent, or deleted through the connector. The worst case is that Claude reads something a person could already have read.
  • On-demand only. The connector does not sit in the background scanning our SharePoint. It only reaches in when a person asks a question that needs it.
  • Revocable at three independent levels. Any user can disconnect their own account; we can switch the connector off for the whole company in Claude; and our Microsoft administrator can withdraw consent on the Microsoft side. Any one of these cuts access immediately.
  • Fully auditable. Because everything runs through Microsoft with each person's identity, our administrator can see what was queried, by whom, and when, in our existing Microsoft logs.

The one risk that is not really about Claude

If we worry that Claude might show someone a sensitive file, the deeper truth is that the person can already open that file today. Claude does not create new access. So the strongest thing we gain from this exercise is a reason to make sure our SharePoint permissions are actually what we think they are. That is good hygiene regardless of Claude.

05
Protecting our crown jewels

How we wall off finance, HR, and manufacturing secrets.

This is the part I researched most carefully, because it is where a wrong assumption could cost us. Here is what actually works, verified against both Anthropic and Microsoft documentation.

What does not work

A common assumption is that we can give Claude access to some SharePoint sites and not others by adjusting Microsoft permissions site by site. Anthropic's own documentation states clearly that this site-by-site approach is not supported for this connector, because the search it uses looks across the whole tenant. So we should not rely on it.

The hard block that does work

Microsoft has a feature called Restricted Content Discovery. When we switch it on for a specific site, that site's content is removed from the company-wide search that Claude depends on. The people who already have access can still open the files directly, but Claude can no longer find them. Microsoft's documentation confirms this is designed for exactly this purpose, and independent testing confirms it stops Claude.

Important cost catch, and the smart way around it

Restricted Content Discovery requires at least one Microsoft Copilot license in our tenant, or a paid add-on. We just cancelled our three Copilot licenses to save about $1,465 per year, so this feature will switch off after June 2 unless we act.

The smart, lowest-cost path is to keep one Copilot license active (about $488 per year) rather than buy a separate add-on for many users. One license unlocks Restricted Content Discovery across the whole tenant. We would still save roughly $980 per year versus the three licenses we had, and gain a real hard block on sensitive sites. I will confirm the exact cheapest route before we commit.

The free layers we add on top

  • Permission hygiene. Because Claude inherits each person's access, the cleanest protection costs nothing: make sure the people who use Claude do not have access to the crown-jewel sites in the first place. If they cannot open it, neither can Claude.
  • A company instruction for Claude. We can give Claude a standing rule never to look at named sensitive sites, even if asked. This is a softer block that relies on Claude following the instruction, but it adds a useful extra layer and costs nothing.
  • Strip the riskiest permissions. During setup we can remove specific permissions, such as access to meeting recordings, without losing the rest of the connector.

Used together, these give us a strong, layered defense around finance, HR, and our manufacturing know-how.

06
Outlook, specifically

What to know about email and the connector.

  • Read-only here too. Claude can read and summarize email but cannot send, reply, forward, or delete. This is actually stricter than the Google version of the connector, which can draft replies.
  • Shared mailboxes are included. If a person has access to a shared mailbox such as info@ or accounts@, Claude can read those messages for them as well. We confirm who has shared-mailbox access during the pilot.
  • Personal inboxes stay personal. Claude only reads the signed-in person's own mail and the shared mailboxes they already use. It cannot read a colleague's private inbox.
  • Archived mail is included. The read permission covers archived messages, not just the current inbox. Worth knowing for anything sensitive sitting in old email.
07
My recommendation

Turn it on carefully, in two phases.

The risk is low to moderate and well understood, and the controls are real. I recommend we proceed, starting small.

Before we switch anything on

  • Decide on the crown-jewel protection: keep one Copilot license to enable the hard block, or rely on permission hygiene and the company instruction for the pilot.
  • Identify the specific finance, HR, and manufacturing sites to wall off.
  • Decide whether to strip the meeting-recording permission from the start.

Phase one, a small pilot (about three weeks)

  • Our IT support partner grants the one-time Microsoft approval and limits the connector to a small pilot group.
  • Pilot group of about five people across the divisions, using it in real work.
  • We watch what works, what surprises us, and what makes anyone uncomfortable.

Phase two, broader rollout (weeks four to eight)

  • Write a short staff usage policy.
  • Run a 30-minute training session per division.
  • Expand access based on what we learned, with the sensitive-site blocks confirmed in place.
Cost to the company

The connector itself is included in our existing plan at no extra cost. The only optional spend is keeping one Copilot license (about $488 per year) to enable the hard block on sensitive sites, which still leaves us saving against what we paid before.
